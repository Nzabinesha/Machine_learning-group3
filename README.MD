Section A: Understanding questions Why Not Just Split at the Mean . 

 
Imagine you're handed a giant list of heights — 1,139 numbers — and someone tells you: "half of these came from fathers, and half came from their children. But I mixed the list up and threw away the labels. Figure out which is which, without me telling you." 

This is what our  assignment is about. We are given a column of numbers. No labels. We are required to figure out that there are two hidden groups inside that single column, and to describe each group mathematically. I mean the average height , variation,  and what fraction of data that belongs to it. 


In this assignment we are given a dataset that contains heights of fathers, mothers, and children. We are assuming that we do not have labels telling us who is who. And we have to tell who is who  by mathematical calculation. We have selected to compare fathers and children.  We are trying to figure out which heights are for fathers and which are for children . 

N.b We used google colab to first visualise data and to understand its basics.

Why can’t we just find the mean of the whole dataset and say that heights below that mean are for children and height above that mean are for fathers? This is very wrong because of the nature of our own dataset. Here is the key facts about our dataset: 
1.The global mean of the pooled dataset is 67.209 inches.
2.If you split there, you get two piles: one averaging 64.26, the other 70.22.
This means the mean of data  < = 67.209 is 64.26 while the mean of data > 67.209 is 70.22.

These facts picture this problem : 50.2% of every single point in the dataset (57 2 out of 1,139 )  falls within just 3 inches of that boundary. To mean  just saying  this belongs to which side is just hard yes or no question. By using this method a person with 67.5 will be declared a father while a person with 67 will be declared a child yet their height is barely distinguished. This method does not take uncertainty into account . 

This highlights the need for another method that can help us in solving this question where we have the mixed data of two different populations blended together and this is where  Expectation-Maximization plays a crucial role. Expectation-Maximization takes uncertainty into account and  finds a probability of the heights to be of father or of child. Like there is 55 % probability that a person with 67.5 height is father and 45% probability that is a child. 

Our answer to the first question is no No, we should not simply split at the mean, because a hard cutoff pretends there's no ambiguity when over half the dataset sits close enough to the boundary to be genuinely ambiguous — EM instead assigns soft, probabilistic memberships that respect that uncertainty and refine themselves iteratively."

Section Using EM to know who is who:

Assumptions made: we assume data was generated by two overlapping bell curves (Gaussian / Normal distributions) stacked on top of each other:
Group 1 ("Children") — has its own average height (μ₁) and its own spread (σ₁²)
Group 2 ("Pros"/Fathers) — has its own average height (μ₂) and its own spread (σ₂²)
π₁ and π₂ — the mixing weights, i.e., what fraction of the whole dataset belongs to each group (these always add up to 1)

## Part 2 — Bayesian Probability (IMDb Movie Reviews)

**Dataset:** IMDb Movie Reviews dataset, as provided for the assignment.

**We calculate only** P(Positive | Keyword), as required.

**Keywords selected:**
- Positive: `excellent`, `amazing`, `great`, `love`
- Negative (identified but not computed): `terrible`, `awful`, `boring`, `worst`

**Justification:** these words carry strong emotional charge and are commonly used to express clear satisfaction or disappointment with a movie, making them reliable sentiment signals.

### Results (real output)

| Keyword | Prior | Posterior |
|---|---|---|
| excellent | 0.50267 | 0.8595 |
| amazing | 0.50267 | 0.7716 |
| love | 0.50267 | 0.68284 |
| great | 0.50267 | 0.65253 |

*(Full likelihood/marginal values for each keyword are printed in the notebook output.)*

**Interpretation:** "excellent" is the strongest positive-sentiment indicator of the four, followed by "amazing," then "love," then "great." "Great" is the weakest indicator because it's common enough to appear across many negative reviews too (e.g. sarcastic or mixed reviews), pulling its posterior closer to the 0.5 baseline.

### Implementation notes

- Pure Python (no ML libraries), as required
- Modular: `load_reviews()`, `compute_prior()`, and a single `bayes_posterior()` function reused across all four keywords — no repeated counting logic per keyword

## Part 3 — Gradient Descent (Manual Calculation)

Handwritten in a separate submitted PDF, one full iteration per group member (4 members → 4 iterations), each showing the chain rule explicitly for both ∂J/∂m and ∂J/∂b.

**Setup:** `y_hat = Xm + b`, X = [[1,3],[4,10]], initial m = [-1, 2], initial b = [1, 1], y = [5, 6], learning rate α = 0.01.

**Loss trend across the 4 iterations:** 61.00 → 6.50 → 2.50 → 2.16 — steadily decreasing, confirming gradient descent is moving m and b in a direction that reduces error. The largest correction happens in the first step; each subsequent step refines further as the parameters approach a better fit.

## Part 4 — Gradient Descent (Code)

Converts the Part 3 manual work into Python, matching every number exactly.

- `predict()`, `mse_cost()` — the model and cost function
- `scipy_gradient()` — generic derivative helper using `scipy.optimize.approx_fprime`, accepts any cost function and parameter vector
- `analytical_gradient()` — the chain-rule gradient from Part 3, used to cross-check the SciPy result at every iteration
- `gradient_descent_step()` — one reusable function performing a single update (forward pass → gradient → parameter update); the training loop calls this function once per iteration instead of repeating the math inline

**Final result after 4 iterations:** m = [-1.3637, 1.1191], b = [1.0568, 0.8949], final MSE = 2.099581 — matching the handwritten Part 3 calculations exactly.
